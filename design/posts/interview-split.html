Interview

The Archive Keeper

A conversation with Somchai P., who has spent thirty years preserving the club's photographic memory — one negative, one print, one story at a time.

Ananda K. May 28, 2025 12 min read

Somchai P. does not call himself an archivist. He calls himself a member — one who happened to never leave. In 1995, as a second-year Engineering student, he walked into the CU Photo Club's darkroom and offered to help organise the growing collection of prints and negatives. Thirty years later, he still arrives every Saturday morning, keys in hand, to open the cabinet where five decades of club history are stored.

How did you first get involved with the club's archive?
It wasn't planned. In the mid-nineties, the club had been around for nearly thirty years, and nobody really knew what to do with all the material. There were boxes of prints in the storage room, envelopes of negatives stacked on shelves, contact sheets spilling out of folders. I was a new member, and I asked if I could look through them. One thing led to another, and I just kept coming back.
He pauses, looking at the cabinet.
What's the oldest photograph in the collection?
A contact sheet from 1967 — the club's founding year. It shows a group of students standing outside the Faculty of Arts building. One of them is holding a Nikon F, which was state of the art at the time. The print is badly faded, but you can still make out the expressions on their faces. They look serious, like they knew they were starting something important.
How has photography changed in the time you've been with the club?
Everything. When I started, film was the only option. The darkroom was the centre of the club — literally, physically, socially. People would spend hours in there, talking about developer temperatures and dodging techniques. Now it's digital, and the conversation has shifted to colour grading and sensor sizes. But the core is the same. The tools change, but the eye doesn't.
"The tools change every few years. The eye takes a lifetime. An archive like this is proof of that — you can see members from the seventies making the same compositional choices that our current members make. Photography is a conversation across time."
What's your favourite item in the archive?
That's like asking which of your photographs is your favourite. But if I had to choose — there's a series from 1982, shot by a member named Prae. It documents the construction of the Bangkok Skytrain, before it was even called that. She climbed construction sites, talked to workers, came back covered in dust. The images are raw — technically imperfect — but they have a kind of urgency that I've never seen matched. She left the club after graduating, but those photographs are still here.
Why does the archive matter?
Because without it, the club is just a group of people who meet once a week. With it, we're part of something continuous. A student who joins today can look at a photograph made fifty years ago and feel connected to that moment. The archive says: you are not the first person to see this city this way. And you won't be the last.
· · ·

The CU Photo Club archive is open to members every Saturday. If you'd like to volunteer with cataloguing or digitisation, speak to Somchai on your next visit.
