The Art of Waiting:
Finding the Frame in Stillness

Ananda K. June 12, 2025 8 min read

The first thing you learn in photography is how to see. The second — the harder lesson — is how to wait. This is not the passive waiting of boredom, but an active, alert stillness. A kind of listening with your eyes.

I remember my first morning with the CU Photo Club. We gathered at Lumphini Park before sunrise, a dozen of us with cameras hanging from our necks like talismans. The air was cool and damp, heavy with the promise of the first light. Our instructor, a third-year student with patient eyes, gave us a single instruction: find a spot and stay there for an hour.

No walking. No chasing shots. Just stand — or sit, or kneel — and watch what happens. I thought it was absurd. I had come to make photographs, not to meditate. But I complied, choosing a bench near the water where the lotus leaves clustered like green palm prints.

The first fifteen minutes

The first quarter of an hour was agony. My thumb hovered over the shutter release. A heron stood motionless at the water's edge — click. A jogger passed through a shaft of amber — click. I was making pictures, but I wasn't seeing. I was collecting, not composing.

Then something shifted. The heron, which I had already photographed, lifted its head. Not to fly — just to turn it slightly, catching the light at a new angle. I raised my camera, then stopped. I watched instead. The bird took one slow step, then another. The jogger came again, this time from the opposite direction. The light had changed colour — from amber to gold to something almost pink.

I hadn't moved. But everything around me had moved, and suddenly there were a hundred photographs where before there had been none.

"The photograph is not made by the camera. It is made by the interval between what you expect and what arrives."

Learning to listen with your eyes

There is a Japanese concept called ma — the interval, the pause, the space between things. In photography, ma is the distance between you and your subject, but also the distance between one frame and the next. It is the breath you take before pressing the shutter. Without it, your images are noise. With it, they become sentences.

At the club, we talk about this constantly. The best photographers on our roster are not the ones with the most expensive gear or the fastest reflexes. They are the ones who know how to wait. They arrive early and leave late. They shoot less but keep more.

"I think of waiting as a kind of focusing — not of the lens, but of the attention. The more still you become, the more the world reveals itself to you." — Mintra S., club member and street photographer

This is not mysticism. It is a practical discipline. Consider: when you stand still in a busy street, people eventually stop noticing you. You become part of the furniture, part of the background. And then the real moments happen — not the posed smiles or the performative gestures, but the unguarded ones. The yawn, the glance, the hand reaching for another hand.

Waiting as presence

In our workshop last semester, we asked members to photograph the same location — a corner of the university campus — every day for a week, for exactly thirty minutes each time. The results were illuminating. Day one yielded frantic, scattered images. Day two was only slightly better. But by day four, something opened up. The compositions grew simpler. The light became a collaborator rather than an obstacle. The photographs began to look like the place actually felt.

What changed was not the location. It was the photographer's relationship to time. By day four, the pressure to produce had dissolved. There was nothing left to prove. The camera became a secondary instrument — the primary one was attention.

The next time you go out to make photographs, try this: find one spot and stay for an hour. Don't leave. Don't scroll through your phone. Just watch. The first fifteen minutes will feel wasteful. The next fifteen will feel uncomfortable. Somewhere in the third quarter, the world will forget you're there. And that is when the photograph arrives.
